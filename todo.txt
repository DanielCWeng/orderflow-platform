1. Market Microstructure & Limit Order Book (LOB) Logic
The Flaw: Market Orders Don't "Sweep" the Book
Look at your consume_ask and consume_bid logic. If an aggressive 250-lot market buy order hits a best ask that only has 50 lots resting, your code deletes the level, creates a new level 1 tick higher, and the remaining 200 lots of the order simply vanish.
Reality: Large orders "sweep the book." A 250-lot buy should consume the 50 lots at $5842.25, then consume 100 lots at $5842.50, and 100 lots at $5842.75. The trade tape should show a sweep, and the price should gap up instantaneously.
Fix: Implement a while size > 0: loop in the consume functions that walks up the LOB, deducting size from L1, then L2, etc., until the order is filled, generating a cluster of trades at different price levels.
The Flaw: Symmetrical Replenishment & Ignored Adverse Selection
When price moves, your replenish() function mean-reverts both the bid and ask sides simultaneously based on mathematical decay.
Reality: Market Makers (MMs) suffer from adverse selection. If a massive wave of market buys comes in, MMs don't just replenish the asks; they cancel (pull) their resting ask limit orders ahead of the price movement to avoid getting run over, and they heavily stack the bids behind the movement to provide support.
Fix: Introduce "book skew." If mkt.recent_moves is positive (uptrend), instantly reduce ask_target by 40% and increase bid_target by 40%. Pull liquidity from the side being attacked.
The Flaw: ES Spread is Almost Always 1 Tick
You have logic to "fix blown spreads > 2 ticks." In the real ES market, a 2-tick spread happens for milliseconds, mostly during news like CPI or FOMC.
Reality: ES is the most liquid equity index future in the world. MMs fiercely compete to be at the inside touch.
Fix: Force the spread to be exactly 1 tick 99% of the time. If the spread ever widens to 2 ticks, the simulation should aggressively step in to fill the gap within the very next tick loop.
2. Trade "Tape" & Order Flow Dynamics
The Flaw: The Ping-Pong Effect (Independent Probabilities)
You determine buy/sell by: is_buy = random.random() < mkt.ou_bias. If the bias is 0.50, you are flipping a coin every trade. The tape will read: BUY, SELL, BUY, BUY, SELL, BUY.
Reality: Order flow is highly autocorrelated (a Hawkes Process). Market orders come in coordinated bursts from algorithms. If a trade is a BUY, the next trade within 50ms has an 80% chance of also being a BUY.
Fix: Add a Markov Chain for trade signs. prob_buy = 0.85 if last_trade == 'BUY' else 0.15. Blend this with your macro ou_bias.
The Flaw: Unrealistic Retail/Institutional Trade Sizing
Your trade distribution caps at 300 lots. While large, raw market orders of 300+ lots do happen, they are rare block trades.
Reality: Institutional players use TWAP/VWAP/Iceberg algorithms. They break a 500-lot order into fifty 10-lot orders fired over 3 seconds.
Fix: Instead of generating one giant 300-lot trade, if your randomizer hits a "large trade", trigger a "Sweep State" that fires 20-40 trades of 5-15 lots exclusively on one side of the book over the next 0.5 seconds.
3. Market Maker Behavior (Spoofing & Icebergs)
The Flaw: Spoofing is Too Far Away
Your spoofing logic places 500-1200 lots 5 to 10 ticks away from the inside market.
Reality: An order 10 ticks away in ES doesn't influence the micro-price because it won't get filled anytime soon. Spoofers place large orders 1 to 3 ticks away to artificially thicken the book and scare HFTs into front-running them, pushing the L1 price.
Fix: Change spoof distance to random.randint(2, 4). Remove it exactly when the price moves within 1 tick of it.
The Flaw: Trailing Icebergs
Your iceberg algorithmically trails the price by 2 ticks.
Reality: Icebergs are usually placed at significant round numbers (e.g., $5840.00) or high-volume nodes. They don't normally trail the touch dynamically tick-by-tick.
Fix: Pick a static round number near the current price and leave the iceberg there until it is fully absorbed or the regime ends.
